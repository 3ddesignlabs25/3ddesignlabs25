About

Building functional hardware where firmware, circuits, and fabrication converge

I design and build embedded systems that solve real problems—devices that operate reliably, integrate cleanly, and work the first time they're powered on. This approach comes from years of hands-on learning, starting with curiosity and evolving into systematic technical expertise.

How I Got Here

I've been working with electronics since I was around ten years old. It started with taking apart old cable boxes and consumer electronics—not to break them, but to understand how they were designed. I'd unscrew the case, examine the circuit board, and study how components were arranged and connected. The first time I successfully reassembled something and it still worked felt like cracking a code. That became the pattern: open it, study it, figure out what each part did, reassemble it, test it.

This wasn't formal education—I learned by breaking things, fixing them, and slowly building an intuition for how electronic systems functioned. I started recognizing voltage regulators, capacitors, logic chips. I began to see patterns in how power was distributed, how signals moved through a board, how designers solved problems with limited space and cost constraints.

As I got older, the projects became more intentional. Arduino boards gave me a platform to write code that controlled hardware directly. ESP32 modules introduced Wi-Fi and wireless communication. Raspberry Pi provided a Linux environment for more complex automation. I learned to solder properly, prototype circuits on breadboards, and design PCBs when projects justified the effort. I taught myself Python for automation and C/C++ for efficient embedded firmware.

3D printing entered the picture when I realized functional enclosures were as important as the electronics inside them. I learned FreeCAD and Tinkercad, designing parts that weren't just protective shells but structural components with mounting points, cable channels, and precise tolerances.

What Makes This Work Different

My approach comes from nearly two decades of hands-on experimentation and systematic problem-solving. I don't separate hardware, firmware, and mechanical design into isolated phases—I develop them concurrently because they influence each other. A circuit isn't done when it works on a breadboard; it's done when it's integrated into an enclosure with proper cable management and mounting hardware. Firmware isn't finished when features work; it's finished when error handling is robust, power consumption is optimized, and the system recovers gracefully from failures. This mindset comes directly from years of learning through iteration: building devices that work in real conditions, not just in testing.
